An Introduction to R

Between 2016 and 2019, I taught an introductory course in R for psychologists, sociologists, and other social scientists at Masaryk University. After four iterations, I decided to turn materials developed in the course into a self-paced introduction to R.

Although anyone can make use of these materials, I believe that those who could benefit the most are students/graduates from fields such as psychology, sociology, or political science. This conclusion stems from knowing my own background and the original target group of the course. I was trained to use IBM SPSS for doing statistical analysis in research, be it descriptive statistics using box-plots or multivariate techniques like linear mixed models. After being able to translate these skills into R, was asked to show a way to others with a similar background.

Currently, the materials are accessible as individual R notebooks - commentary along with demonstrations of the respective code. In future, I am going to turn the content in a handbook. That being said, enjoy the journey!

Generative art

Quite recently, I began to appreciate a unique domain intersecting the worlds of coding and art - generative art. To simplify things a lot, generative art represents the involvement of a human-independent system (such as an algorithm) in a creative process. Such a system is a key element within the creative process, including its outputs. Be it paintings, songs, novels, or sculptures. If you would like to have a better overview, the following article on Wikipedia could be a place to start.

Once you know what to expect, take a peek at “my” pieces if you like.
